How to Find a Personal Loan in Canada in 2026 (And What Lenders Are Actually Looking For)

Whether you're consolidating debt, covering an unexpected bill, or planning a big purchase — here's what you need to know before applying for a personal loan in Canada.

SR

Sarah Reynolds

Personal Finance Writer · 8 years covering Canadian lending

Personal loans are one of the most flexible financial tools available to Canadians. Unlike a mortgage or car loan, a personal loan can be used for almost anything — paying down credit card debt, funding home repairs, covering medical costs, or simply building a financial cushion.

But navigating the lending landscape in Canada can be confusing, especially if you're not sure where to start or what lenders actually look for. This guide breaks it down clearly.

What Is a Personal Loan?

A personal loan is a fixed amount of money borrowed from a bank, credit union, or online lender, repaid over a set period (usually 1–5 years) with regular monthly payments. Most personal loans in Canada are unsecured, meaning you don't need to put up collateral like your home or car.

Loan amounts typically range from $500 to $50,000, depending on your credit profile and the lender.

What Do Lenders Look At?

When you apply for a personal loan, most Canadian lenders evaluate a few key factors:

  • Credit score — Most lenders prefer a score of 600 or above, though some work with lower scores.
  • Income and employment — Lenders want to see you can reliably make payments.
  • Debt-to-income ratio — How much of your monthly income already goes toward debt.
  • Loan purpose — Some lenders offer better rates for specific uses like debt consolidation.

Good to know: Many online lenders in Canada now offer soft credit checks, meaning you can see your estimated rate and eligibility without any impact to your credit score. This makes it much easier to shop around before committing.

Common Reasons Canadians Take Out Personal Loans

There's no single "right" reason to take out a personal loan. Here are the most common situations where Canadians find them useful:

  • Debt consolidation — Combining multiple high-interest debts into one lower monthly payment.
  • Home improvements — Renovations, repairs, or upgrades that add value to your home.
  • Vehicle expenses — Repairs, a used car purchase, or covering costs between vehicles.
  • Unexpected bills — Medical, dental, or emergency expenses that weren't planned for.
  • Life events — Weddings, moving costs, or other major milestones.

How to Compare Loan Offers

Not all personal loans are created equal. When comparing options, pay close attention to:

  • Annual Percentage Rate (APR) — This is the true cost of the loan, including fees.
  • Loan term — Shorter terms mean higher monthly payments but less interest overall.
  • Prepayment penalties — Can you pay it off early without a fee?
  • Funding speed — Some lenders can deposit funds within 24–48 hours.

Tips Before You Apply

A few things that can improve your chances of approval and help you get a better rate:

  • Check your credit report for free at Equifax or TransUnion before applying.
  • Pay down existing credit card balances if possible — this lowers your utilization ratio.
  • Apply to lenders who do soft checks first so you can compare without hurting your score.
  • Have your employment information and recent pay stubs ready.
